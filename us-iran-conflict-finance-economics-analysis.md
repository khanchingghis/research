## Executive Summary

The US-Iran military conflict, which escalated dramatically in late February 2026 with coordinated US-Israeli strikes on Iranian territory ("Operation Epic Fury"), has triggered the most significant geopolitical shock to global financial markets since Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022. The near-complete shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil supply (13-20 million barrels per day) transits — has created an energy supply crisis with cascading effects across every major asset class and economy.

As of mid-March 2026, the conflict has produced:
- **Oil prices** surging 16-26% from pre-conflict levels
- **Equities** declining ~3% from January highs
- **Treasury yields** rising sharply on inflation fears rather than falling on safe-haven demand
- **The US dollar** strengthening ~2% against major currencies
- **Gold** reaching an all-time high of $5,246/oz before settling in a volatile range
- **Global GDP growth** forecasts revised downward by 0.1-0.4 percentage points depending on oil price trajectory

The conflict represents a classic stagflationary shock: simultaneously raising inflation expectations and lowering growth prospects, putting central banks — particularly the Federal Reserve — in an extraordinarily difficult position.

## Oil Markets and Energy Supply Disruption

### Price Action

The oil market has experienced the sharpest supply-driven price shock since the 1973 Arab oil embargo in proportional terms of supply at risk.

- **Brent crude:** Surged from ~$73 to $85-92/bbl (16-26% increase)
- **WTI crude:** Rose to $81.01/bbl (up 8.51%)
- **Diesel:** Up ~10%
- **Gasoline:** Up ~4%

### Supply Fundamentals

The Strait of Hormuz closure has removed an unprecedented volume of oil from global markets:

- **Gulf production cut:** At least 10 million barrels per day shut in
- **Iraqi production:** Down ~1.5 million bbl/day due to storage and export constraints
- **Qatar LNG:** Production halted entirely
- **Regional refining:** 3+ million bbl/day of capacity offline

### Price Forecasts

| Source | Scenario | Brent Forecast |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| Goldman Sachs | Base case (Mar-Apr 2026) | $98/bbl |
| Goldman Sachs | Year-end 2026 | $71/bbl |
| Goldman Sachs | Severe disruption | $110/bbl |
| Futures curve | Normalization | Pre-war levels not until Aug 2027 |
| Iran military rhetoric | Extreme scenario | $200/bbl |

### Emergency Response

The IEA's unanimous decision to release 400 million barrels from strategic petroleum reserves on March 11 was the largest coordinated release in history. However, this covers only approximately 20 days of disrupted supply, underscoring the fragility of the situation if the Strait remains closed.

## Equity Markets

### Broad Market Performance

- **S&P 500:** Down ~1.4% since strikes began; ~3% below January 2026 record high
- **Single-day drops:** Up to 0.9% on escalation days
- The sell-off has been measured relative to historical wartime drawdowns, partly because markets had already priced in some geopolitical risk premium

### Market Rotation

A significant rotation has occurred away from growth/technology toward value-oriented and defensive sectors. The conflict has interrupted what were developing bullish growth expectations for 2026, with nearly 60% of CEOs citing geopolitical tensions as a high disruption risk.

## Fixed Income and Treasury Markets

The Treasury market response has been notably unusual for a geopolitical crisis:

- **10-year yield:** Rose to 4.23% (up 24 bps from late February)
- **30-year yield:** Reached 4.9%

### Why Bonds Sold Off Instead of Rallying

Typically, Treasuries rally (yields fall) during geopolitical crises as investors seek safety. The opposite has occurred because:

1. **Inflation shock fears dominate:** Rising oil prices are expected to feed through into broader inflation, making fixed-income returns less attractive
2. **Fed rate cut expectations pushed out:** Markets now see the Fed holding rates higher for longer to combat oil-driven inflation
3. **Stagflation concerns:** The combination of higher inflation and lower growth creates the worst possible environment for duration-heavy bond portfolios
4. **Fiscal concerns:** The estimated $831 million per 24 hours in military costs adds to deficit worries

This bond sell-off concurrent with equity weakness — a rare "stocks and bonds down together" scenario — signals deep investor anxiety about the inflationary path ahead.

## Currency Markets

### US Dollar

The dollar has strengthened approximately 1.5-2% against major currencies since the conflict began, with the DXY index reaching 100.35. Two factors drive this:

1. **Safe-haven demand:** The dollar remains the world's primary reserve currency and benefits during crises
2. **US energy independence:** As a net energy exporter, the US is relatively insulated from the oil shock compared to energy-importing economies

### Major Currency Pairs

| Currency | Move vs USD | Driver |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| EUR/USD | Down ~2% to $1.14-1.15 | European recession fears from energy costs |
| JPY | Down 1.5-3% | Japan's near-total energy import dependence |
| INR | Down 1.5-3% | India's heavy oil import reliance |
| KRW | Down 1.5-3% | South Korea's energy import exposure |

### Outlook

Despite near-term strength, Reuters-polled FX strategists expect the dollar rally to be short-lived, with most still predicting dollar weakness by year-end 2026 if the conflict resolves.

## Gold and Safe-Haven Assets

### Price Action

Gold reached a historic all-time high of **$5,246/oz** in March 2026, up ~14% from ~$4,600 in January. However, the path has been volatile:

- Initial surge from $5,296 to $5,423 immediately post-strikes
- Sharp 6% decline to $5,085 on March 3
- Subsequent trading range of $5,050-$5,200

### Counterintuitive Dynamics

Gold's failure to sustain a war-driven rally is explained by several forces:

1. **Dollar strength:** A rising dollar makes gold more expensive in other currencies, suppressing non-US demand
2. **Rising Treasury yields:** Higher yields increase the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold
3. **Forced liquidations:** Geopolitical shocks trigger margin calls and forced selling across asset classes, including gold
4. **Inflation-rate expectations:** If oil-driven inflation forces higher interest rates, the real yield on bonds may compress gold's appeal

### Structural Bull Case

Despite short-term headwinds, major banks remain bullish:
- **J.P. Morgan:** Targets $6,300/oz by end-2026
- **Deutsche Bank:** Targets $6,000/oz by end-2026
- **Central bank buying:** Over 1,100 tons purchased in 2025, supporting de-dollarization trends

## Macroeconomic Impact

### Inflation

Goldman Sachs estimates the following per sustained 10% increase in oil prices:
- **Headline PCE inflation:** +0.2 percentage points
- **Core PCE inflation:** +0.04 percentage points

At $100/bbl oil (vs ~$73 pre-conflict), the impact scales to:
- **Global headline inflation:** +0.7 percentage points
- **Global GDP growth:** -0.4 percentage points

### Federal Reserve Dilemma

The Fed faces what Bloomberg characterized as an "impossible position":

- **Inflationary pressure:** Oil-driven cost increases argue for maintaining or raising rates
- **Growth headwinds:** Geopolitical uncertainty and higher energy costs argue for easing
- **Political pressure:** Presidential demands for rate cuts conflict with the inflation mandate
- **Market expectations:** Rate cut expectations have been pushed further out since the conflict began

This is the classic stagflation trap, where the central bank cannot simultaneously address both rising prices and slowing growth.

### US Economic Resilience

The US is better positioned than most economies due to:
- Domestic oil and gas production providing a buffer
- Net energy exporter status
- Dollar's reserve currency role attracting capital flows
- Diversified economy less dependent on energy-intensive manufacturing

## Regional Economic Fallout

### Europe
- **Risk:** Recession. Sustained higher energy prices directly threaten an already fragile recovery
- **Vulnerability:** Heavy dependence on imported energy, especially natural gas (Qatar LNG disruption compounds this)
- **Euro weakness:** EUR/USD forecasts revised down to 1.14-1.15

### China
- **Compound stress:** Loss of discounted Iranian oil imports adds to existing economic strain from US tariffs and real estate sector collapse
- **Energy dependency:** Heavily reliant on Middle Eastern oil transiting the Strait of Hormuz
- **84% of Hormuz-transited oil** goes to Asia (China, India, Japan, South Korea)

### Middle East (Non-Combatant)
- **Iraq:** 1.5 million bbl/day production cuts due to inability to export
- **Qatar:** Complete LNG production shutdown
- **Gulf States:** Massive revenue and production disruptions despite not being direct combatants

### Emerging Markets
- Energy-importing emerging markets face triple pressure: higher import bills, currency depreciation, and reduced global growth demand for their exports

## Predictions and Next Steps

### Near-Term (March-June 2026)

1. **Conflict Duration:** Both the US and Iran have maintained hardline stances and rejected negotiations. Analysts warn of prospects for protracted conflict. The military campaign requires several more weeks to complete its stated objective of degrading Iran's ability to project power regionally. The war has evolved into attritional warfare centered on drones and interceptor missiles.

2. **Oil Prices:** Goldman Sachs expects Brent to average ~$98/bbl through March-April 2026. If the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, prices could spike to $110+ and the IEA's 400 million barrel strategic reserve release will be exhausted within ~20 days. The key variable is whether the Strait reopens to commercial shipping.

3. **Equities:** Expect continued volatility with a downward bias. The S&P 500 likely trades in a range of -5% to -10% from January highs if the conflict persists. Defense and energy stocks will continue to outperform. Any ceasefire or diplomatic breakthrough would trigger a sharp relief rally.

4. **Fed Policy:** The Federal Reserve is likely to hold rates steady, caught between inflation pressures and growth risks. Rate cuts are off the table for H1 2026 barring a rapid conflict resolution. Markets should price in "higher for longer."

5. **Inflation:** US headline CPI likely accelerates 0.3-0.5 percentage points above pre-conflict trajectory if oil remains above $90/bbl. Core inflation impact is more contained but still meaningful.

### Medium-Term (H2 2026)

6. **Diplomatic Resolution Probability:** Despite current hardline stances, economic costs are mounting on all sides. A negotiated ceasefire or de-escalation becomes more likely by mid-2026 as:
   - US military costs escalate (>$830 million/day)
   - Global recession risks mount, creating diplomatic pressure from allies
   - Iran's new leadership (post-Khamenei) may seek a pragmatic exit
   - Strategic reserve depletion forces urgency

7. **Oil Price Normalization:** If conflict de-escalates, Goldman Sachs forecasts Brent declining to $71/bbl by year-end 2026. However, futures markets suggest pre-war levels may not return until August 2027, reflecting lasting infrastructure damage and risk premiums.

8. **European Recession Risk:** If energy prices remain elevated through Q2-Q3 2026, Europe likely enters a mild recession. The ECB may be forced to cut rates despite elevated inflation — a politically difficult decision.

9. **US Dollar Peak and Decline:** The dollar is expected to peak in Q2 2026 and weaken in H2 as conflict resolution removes safe-haven demand and relative US economic advantage narrows.

### Long-Term Structural Shifts

10. **Energy Security Realignment:** The conflict will permanently accelerate:
    - Investment in non-OPEC oil production
    - Renewable energy and nuclear capacity buildout
    - Strategic petroleum reserve replenishment globally
    - Diversification of energy supply routes away from Hormuz dependence

11. **De-Globalization of Energy:** Countries will increasingly prioritize energy self-sufficiency, potentially fragmenting global energy markets into regional blocs.

12. **Defense Spending Cycle:** A new multi-year defense spending cycle is likely across NATO and allied nations, benefiting the defense industrial base but adding to fiscal pressures.

13. **Gold's Structural Bull Market:** Central bank buying, de-dollarization trends, and recurring geopolitical shocks support gold reaching $6,000-6,300/oz by late 2026 and potentially higher in subsequent years.

14. **Iran's Post-Conflict Economy:** Regardless of military outcome, Iran faces years of economic reconstruction. The succession of leadership adds political uncertainty that will keep Iranian assets deeply discounted and complicate any sanctions relief framework.
